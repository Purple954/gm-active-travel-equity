Spatial Analysis

The most deprived parts of Greater Manchester walk the most, but walking here is a symptom of poverty

A neighbourhood-level analysis of cycling infrastructure, deprivation, and travel behaviour across the ten GM boroughs finds that active travel is highest where poverty is deepest, driven by residents who cannot afford to do otherwise.

Data: OpenStreetMap · IMD 2019 · Census 2021 TS061 · ONS LSOA Boundaries  ·  Method: Spatial join · OLS with borough fixed effects
The paradox

High active travel in deprived areas looks like success. It is not.

Across Greater Manchester's 1,702 Lower Super Output Areas, the most deprived neighbourhoods have the highest rates of active travel. In the most deprived quintile, 12.5% of working residents travel actively. In the least deprived, 4.9%. On the surface this looks like equity. The data tells a different story.

Walking accounts for nearly all of it. The correlation between deprivation and walking is strong (Spearman ρ = 0.71, p < 0.001). The correlation between deprivation and cycling is much weaker (ρ = 0.31). And the relationship between cycling infrastructure density and active travel largely disappears once borough-level factors are accounted for.

People in the most deprived parts of Greater Manchester walk because they cannot afford to drive or take public transport. High walking rates in these areas reflect transport poverty, and pointing to them as evidence of good provision misreads the mechanism entirely. These are areas where people walk despite poor conditions.

Key findings

What this means for transport investment in Greater Manchester

  • The deprivation-walking relationship (ρ = 0.71) reflects cost barriers to car ownership and transit use. High walking rates in deprived areas are a signal of constrained choice, and transport policy should treat them as such.
  • Once borough fixed effects are included, cycling infrastructure density loses statistical significance (p = 0.196). The city-centre geography of Manchester and Salford creates a correlation that disappears under proper controls.
  • Borough identity matters as much as deprivation. Manchester (+2.8pp) and Salford (+3.2pp) have substantially higher active travel at equivalent deprivation levels. Local policies, urban form, and network connectivity drive large residual differences that deprivation alone cannot explain.
  • Areas with high walking rates and poor conditions represent the greatest opportunity for mode shift from walking to cycling, at lower behaviour-change cost than starting from a car-dependent baseline.
  • Standard cost-benefit frameworks assess routes on projected trip volumes. In deprived areas, suppressed demand means low trip volumes even on routes where the need is greatest. Without an explicit equity weighting, investment will systematically favour already-mobile areas.
